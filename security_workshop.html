Passwords: It's (Almost) All Math

Part One

The Math

A password of any given length (L) built using a character set of size (N - which represents complexity) creates a combination of posibilities where:

Possible Combinations = NL

As the English alphabet contains 26 characters, if we have a password length of 8 alphabetical characters (only including lowercase letters) then the complexity becomes:

Possible Combinations = 268 = 208,827,064,576

The mathematical expression explains why password length and not simply complexity make the biggest impact. Complexity is the base and password length is the exponent.

The following table exemplifies how simply changing complexity will affect the possible combinations for a password with a length of 8 characters:

Characters (Length = 8) Complexity (N) Possible Combinations
Lowercase letters only 26 208,827,064,576
Lowercase letters + Uppercase letters 52 53,459,728,531,456
Lowercase + Uppercase letters + Numbers 62 218,340,105,584,896
All letters + Numbers + Special Characters ~95 6,634,204,312,890,625


The addition of complexity does cause the number of possibilities to increase, however, not to the same magnitude as when we increase the length of the password:

Part Two

What Is Actually Stored

When you type your password into a website, what does that website keep?

A well-built system never stores your password. It stores a hash of your password.

What's a Hash?

→ shred & fry
If we start with a Yukon Gold potato that is about the size of a fist and has a brown spot on the left side, put that potato through physical hashing (turning it into a hashbrown) the person eating that hashbrown has no way of taking the parts of the hashbrown and reconstructing the same potato exactly as it was. This is the idea behind password hashing.


The system takes your password, runs it through a hashing process, and stores what comes out: a fixed-length string of characters that reveals nothing about what went in. This breaks down in the real-world because we can obviously make hashbrowns without knowing what variety of potatoes were used, their size, and characteristics do not matter. With passwords, the details (characters used, length) all matter and are part of the hashing process.

Here is that consistency in real numbers. These are actual Secure Hash Algorithm 256-bit (SHA-256) hashes:

What is typed (the potato) What the system stores (the hashbrown)
password 5e884898da28047151d0e56f8dc6292773603d0d6aabbdd62a11ef721d1542d8
Password e7cf3ef4f17c3999a94f2c6f612e8a888e5b1026878e4e19398b23bd38ec221a
password1 0b14d501a594442a01c6859541bcb3e8164d183d32937b851835442f69d5c94e


One letter changed from lowercase to uppercase, and the output is unrecognizable. There is no partial credit and no getting warmer. Note also that every hashbrown is exactly the same size, no matter how big the potato was.

So how does logging in work? When you set your password, the system shreds it and files away the hash of your password. When you come back and type your password, the system shreds what you just typed and compares the two hashes. If they match, you are in. The system verified your password without ever knowing what it was. It only ever had the hash.

In The Wild

A breach does not hand an attacker your password. It hands them a database of hashes (usually millions of them when major compromises occur).

The attackers do not have your password, but they now have something to test against.

That is what "cracking a password" actually means. It is guessing, at enormous speed, against a stolen file. And because the attacker works offline on their own hardware, there is nothing to slow them down: no login page, no lockout after three tries, no delay. Just a graphics card working as fast as it physically can on the order of billions to hundreds of billions per second (depending on the hardware and hashing algorithms used).

Which brings us straight back to Part One. The only thing standing between an attacker and your password is how many hashes they have to try. Unfortunately, math is really important.

Part Three

How the Guessing Actually Happens

Attackers do not guess randomly, and they do not guess in the order you would expect. There are three classic strategies, tried roughly in order of how cheap they are.


1. Brute Force: Try Everything, In Order

The unsubtle approach. Start at aaaaaaaa, hash it, compare. Then aaaaaaab. Then aaaaaaac. Continue through every combination the character set allows until something matches.

There is no cleverness here at all — only speed. A single modern graphics card makes billions of guesses per second, and a purpose-built rig reaches roughly 100 billion per second against a fast hash. That is the number the Password Lab uses.

Brute force is the attack that the NL math from Part One defeats directly. Every character you add multiplies the attacker's work by the size of the character set, exponentially. This is why the 16-character row in that first table is roughly 200 billion times larger than the 8-character row. Past about 16 characters, exhaustive guessing stops being worth attempting — the universe is not old enough.

✓What defeats it: length. Nothing else, and nothing else is needed.


2. Dictionary Attack: Try What People Actually Pick

This is the attack that breaks most real passwords, and it is the reason Part One is only almost the whole story.

Brute force treats every 8-character string as equally likely. But people do not pick random strings — they pick words. So instead of counting up from aaaaaaaa, the attacker starts with a list: dictionary words, names, sports teams, cities, and most damaging of all, hundreds of millions of real passwords harvested from previous breaches.

Then the software applies rules, automatically: capitalize the first letter. Append a year. Append an exclamation mark. Swap a for @, o for 0, i for 1. Try every combination of those.

Here is the uncomfortable part. P@ssw0rd! feels clever. To a cracking tool it is one dictionary word plus three rules the software already knows. It is not a needle in a haystack. It is near the top of the list.

Compare two passwords of identical length and identical character variety:

Password Length Guesses actually needed
Redhawks1 9 A few thousand falls instantly
q7Wm#kZ2p 9 Roughly 570 trillion holds for a while


Same length. Same character sets. Wildly different outcomes — because one lives in a search space of thousands and the other lives in a search space of trillions. Length only protects you when the attacker has to search the whole space.

✓What defeats it: unpredictability, not decoration. Symbol substitutions buy you almost nothing. A passphrase of unrelated words — copper-lantern-breeze-moss — is long and unguessable, which is the combination that works.


3. Rainbow Table: Do the Work Once, Look It Up Forever

Hashing takes time. If an attacker is going to crack thousands of stolen hashes, why hash the word password over and over for each one?

They do not. They compute the hashes for millions of likely passwords in advance, store the results in an enormous precomputed lookup structure, and then simply look up each stolen hash to see which password produced it. Cracking stops being a computation and becomes a lookup — and a lookup is close to instantaneous.

This works precisely because hashing is deterministic. The word password hashes to 5e884898… on every system on earth, forever. The same potato always makes the same hashbrown. Which means a table built years ago still works against a database stolen tomorrow.


The countermeasure is salting. A salt is a unique random value the system adds to your password before hashing it. If your salt is x7f2, the system does not hash password — it hashes passwordx7f2, producing an entirely different hash.

In kitchen terms: every account gets its own secret seasoning mixed in before shredding. Two employees who both chose password now have completely different stored hashes. Every precomputed table in the world becomes useless, because none of them were built with your salt. The attacker is forced back to guessing one password at a time — right back into the math from Part One.

!What defeats it: salting — but that is the system's job, not yours. You cannot salt someone else's database, and plenty of older systems never bothered. What you control is blast radius: if that unsalted forum from 2014 gets breached, a unique password means it costs you one account instead of all of them.


And One That Needs No Cracking At All

Credential stuffing. An attacker takes an email and password that already leaked from one breached site and simply tries the pair on a hundred others. Nothing is guessed. Nothing is reversed. No hash is broken. The password is just correct.

Against this, password strength is completely irrelevant. Your strongest possible password is defeated the moment you use it in two places. Only uniqueness helps — and multi-factor authentication, which stops the attacker even when the password is right.


Putting It Together

No single habit defends against all four. This is why the advice is a short list rather than one rule.

Attack What it beats What defeats it
Brute force Short passwords, however complex Length — 16+ characters
Dictionary Words, names, dates, symbol swaps Unpredictability
Rainbow table Unsalted databases, reused passwords Salting + uniqueness
Credential stuffing Any password used more than once Uniqueness + MFA

Lab 3

What to carry out the door

Passwords

  • Length wins. A long passphrase of ordinary words beats a short string of symbols. Aim for 16+ characters.
  • Never reuse. One breached site shouldn't unlock the rest of your life. A password manager makes unique passwords effortless.
  • Turn on multi-factor authentication. Even a cracked password is useless without the second factor.
  • Don't build passwords from public facts — names, mascots, the current year, the season. Attackers guess these first.

Email & phishing

  • Check the actual sender address, not just the display name. Hover before you trust.
  • Urgency is a weapon. "Act now or lose access" is designed to stop you thinking. Slow down.
  • Hover over links to see where they really go. When in doubt, navigate to the site yourself instead of clicking.
  • Verify money and data requests through a second channel — a phone call, an in-person check especially if the "sender" is a supervisor.
  • Report, don't just delete. Forwarding a phish to IT protects the colleague who gets it next.
